The Great King Pururavas

Pururavas was the first truly great mortal monarch of the Lunar Dynasty. He was extraordinarily handsome, brave, learned, and just. His kingdom extended across the fertile Ganga plains. The Bhagavata Purana (IX.14.15) describes him as "equal to the splendour of the sun, generous as the earth, patient as the ocean, and swift as the wind." He performed great yajnas (sacrifices) and was beloved by his subjects.

The Love Story of Pururavas and Urvashi

Pururavas fell desperately in love with Urvashi â€” the most exquisite of all Apsaras (celestial dancers), who had been sent from Indra's court to the mortal world. Urvashi agreed to live with Pururavas as his wife under three conditions: (1) he must never appear before her without proper clothing; (2) she had two rams she loved, which must always be protected; (3) she would eat only ghee (clarified butter).

They lived together in divine happiness for many years, bearing six sons. But the Gandharvas, jealous of Urvashi's time on earth, plotted to break the conditions. One night they stole the two rams. Urvashi cried out. Pururavas leaped up to chase the thieves â€” naked, forgetting the condition. In that moment, the Gandharvas flashed lightning, illuminating Pururavas in his nakedness. Urvashi, having witnessed this violation of the condition, was bound by cosmic law to depart. She left immediately.

The Reunion and the Fire

Pururavas, mad with grief, wandered the earth searching for Urvashi. He eventually found her at the Kurukshetra lake. She agreed to meet him once a year. During one of these meetings, the Gandharvas offered Pururavas a boon â€” he could choose to become a Gandharva and live with Urvashi in heaven. Pururavas agreed. They gave him a heavenly fire. The Fall of Nahusha â€” Pride Before the Great Sage

As Indra, Nahusha became intoxicated with power. This encounter (described in the Mahabharata Vana Parva, Chapters 177-179) is one of the most philosophically rich episodes in the epic. Yayati â€” The Chakravarti Samrat

Yayati was the greatest emperor of the Chandravansha before the age of Krishna. The story of Yayati's two wives is one of the most elaborate and morally complex narratives in all of Puranic literature. The two women were close companions but their rivalry led to a dramatic incident where Sharmishtha â€” of higher royal birth â€” humiliated Devayani and threw her into a well.

When she told her father Shukracharya what had happened, Shukracharya â€” the most powerful Brahmin of his age â€” was furious at Sharmishtha.
