The Night of the Birth

The Bhagavata Purana (X.3) describes the birth night with extraordinary poetic power. As the hour of midnight approached, all of nature responded: the rivers ran clear, the forests bloomed, the sky filled with stars, celestial drums sounded, the gandharvas sang. Kamsa's sentries fell into divine sleep. The prison chains of Vasudeva and Devaki fell away on their own. The prison doors opened by themselves.
Vasudeva lifted the newborn Krishna ‚Äî radiant, four-armed, bearing the conch, discus, mace, and lotus, wearing the Kaustubha jewel, adorned with a peacock feather ‚Äî and carried him through the flooded Yamuna river. The Yamuna parted to let him pass. A multi-hooded serpent (Shesha Naga) spread its hoods over the child as an umbrella in the monsoon rain. Vasudeva placed the baby in Gokul, with the cowherd chief Nanda Maharaja and his wife Yashoda ‚Äî and returned to the prison with their newborn daughter.

Krishna as Yadav ‚Äî Not Despite, But Because

A profound question: why did the supreme divine choose to incarnate specifically as a Yadav? The Bhagavatam's answer is multilayered. First, the Yadavs' freedom from the ego of kingship (resulting from Yayati's curse on Yadu) meant Krishna could be born into a community that was proud but not power-obsessed ‚Äî a community where love and bhakti, not political calculation, were the primary values.
Second, the pastoral nature of the Abhira-Yadavs of Gokul ‚Äî their life among cows, forests, rivers ‚Äî provided the perfect environment for an avatar whose fundamental message was love as the highest spiritual path. Krishna's childhood in Gokul, surrounded by simple cowherd folk who loved him purely, without knowledge of his divinity, demonstrates the deepest form of divine-human relationship: love that knows no calculation.
Third, the warrior tradition of the Vrishni Yadavs ‚Äî from which Vasudeva came ‚Äî gave Krishna the martial context in which the Bhagavad Gita's deepest teachings about action, duty, and detachment could be delivered. Without the Yadav warrior-heritage, there would be no battlefield, no Arjuna's crisis, no Gita.

The Bhagavata Purana (X.50, X.69) describes Dwarka in extraordinary detail. The city was built on an island 12 leagues in circumference, surrounded by the ocean and protected by Vishwakarma's divine engineering. It had wide, straight avenues (one of the earliest planned cities described in ancient literature), 900,000 royal palaces decorated with crystal, gold, and jewels, public gardens and parks, assembly halls for the Yadav council (the Sudharma Sabha ‚Äî the original deliberative assembly), and temples to all the major deities.

The Sudharma Sabha ‚Äî Yadav Democracy

One of the most remarkable institutions at Dwarka was the Sudharma Sabha (‡§∏‡•Å‡§ß‡§∞‡•ç‡§Æ‡§æ ‡§∏‡§≠‡§æ) ‚Äî the great assembly hall of the Yadavs. The Bhagavata records that this assembly hall was itself a divine gift from Indra and had the special property that anyone who entered it would never feel hunger, thirst, or exhaustion. But its true significance is political: the Sudharma Sabha was a deliberative council in which the major Yadav clans were represented and major decisions were made collectively. This is one of the earliest documented examples of representative governance in the ancient world. Yadav democracy predates Greek democracy by centuries.
The historical existence of Dwarka is supported by archaeological finds off the coast of modern Dwarka (Okha, Gujarat). Underwater excavations led by the National Institute of Oceanography and the Marine Archaeology Centre have discovered stone structures, anchors, and artefacts dated to approximately 1500 BCE or earlier ‚Äî consistent with a major port city of the late Vedic period. The discovery of a 9,500-year-old city off the Gulf of Khambhat has also been connected by some researchers to the Dwarka tradition.

The Architect of History

Krishna's Role in the Mahabharata ‚Äî The Supreme Strategist

Although Krishna declared he would not lift a weapon in the Mahabharata war, he was its supreme architect. Every major turning point in the 18-day war involved his guidance, his wisdom, or his direct divine intervention. The Mahabharata would have been impossible without the Yadav.

Krishna's Key Roles in the Mahabharata
  • The Peace Negotiator ‚Äî Before the war, Krishna went to Hastinapura as the Pandavas' ambassador and made one final effort for peace, asking Duryodhana for just five villages for the five Pandavas. Duryodhana refused. Only then did Krishna declare war inevitable ‚Äî making clear that every attempt at peace had been exhausted.
  • The Revealer of the Bhagavad Gita ‚Äî On the first morning of the war, when Arjuna collapsed in grief, Krishna spoke the 700 verses of the Bhagavad Gita ‚Äî rearming Arjuna not with a bow but with wisdom. This was the war's most decisive act.
  • The Tactician ‚Äî At every critical juncture, Krishna provided tactical guidance: how to break the Chakravyuha formation, when to violate the rules of warfare to kill Drona (through the deception about Ashwatthama's death), how to neutralise Karna's boons.
  • The Divine Protector ‚Äî When Ashwatthama launched the Brahmastra against the Pandava women and the unborn child Parikshit, Krishna personally entered the womb to protect Parikshit ‚Äî ensuring the continuation of the Pandava line and thus the preservation of the Yadav story in posterity.
  • The Narayani Sena ‚Äî The 18 akshauhini of Yadav warriors (the Narayani Sena) that Krishna lent to Duryodhana ‚Äî while he himself went to the Pandavas ‚Äî were the greatest army ever assembled. The fact that the Pandavas chose the man over the army shows the supreme value of a Yadav's personal wisdom over material force.
  • The Post-War Counsellor ‚Äî After the war, Krishna counselled Yudhishthira through his grief and guilt, visited the dying Bhishma to have him deliver the Shanti Parva and Anushasana Parva teachings, and helped establish the new age of dharmic governance.

The Submersion of Dwarka ‚Äî And What It Means

Thirty-six years after the Mahabharata war, the Yadav age came to a close ‚Äî not through defeat, but through divine completion. The events are recorded in the Bhagavata Purana (Skandha XI) and Mahabharata (Mausala Parva) with great detail and extraordinary philosophical depth.

The Final Days of the Yadavs
The Curse: Samba (Krishna's son) and some young Yadavs played a prank on the visiting sages Vishwamitra, Kanva, and Narada ‚Äî dressing Samba as a pregnant woman and asking what child he would deliver. The furious sages cursed: "He will deliver a mace, and that mace will destroy your entire clan." The mace appeared the next day; Ugrasena ordered it ground to dust and thrown into the sea.

The Signs: In the 36th year after the war, dark omens appeared over Dwarka: whirlwinds, unseasonable lightning, black crows attacking white pigeons, mice devouring everyone's hair and shoelaces, conches, bows and chariots trembling on their own.

The End: Krishna organised the entire population to go on pilgrimage to Prabhasa tirth. There, the Yadavs feasted and drank. The remnants of the powdered mace ‚Äî washed ashore as sharp grass (eraka) ‚Äî became weapons in a drunken quarrel. The Yadavs destroyed each other with that sacred grass. Krishna himself allowed it ‚Äî for it was the universe completing its cycle.
Krishna's Departure and Dwarka's Submersion
After all the Yadavs had departed, Krishna sat in meditation at Bhalka Tirth near Somnath. A hunter named Jara (jara = old age) mistook his lotus-red foot for a bird behind a bush and shot an arrow. Krishna called Jara to him, forgave him completely, and sent him to the highest heaven for having touched God's feet ‚Äî even accidentally. Then Krishna departed from his mortal form.

Simultaneously, the beautiful city of Dwarka ‚Äî with all its 900,000 palaces, its gardens, its streets, its Sudharma Sabha ‚Äî was submerged beneath the ocean waves, exactly as Varuna had stipulated when he gave the land: it would be reclaimed when God departed.

The Yadav legacy dispersed: the surviving Yadavs (the women and elderly who had not gone to Prabhasa) were escorted by Arjuna northward. Some settled in Punjab, some in Indraprastha, others spread across India ‚Äî carrying the Yadav traditions, the bhakti, the music, the cow-herding culture ‚Äî into the fabric of Indian civilization.
The submersion of Dwarka is not a tragedy ‚Äî it is a completion. The Bhagavata Purana frames it with absolute clarity: the Yadavs had accomplished their cosmic purpose. They had given birth to and sheltered the greatest avatar in human history. Their work was done. The physical city submerging simply meant that Dwarka's eternal form passed from the material to the transcendental ‚Äî where it remains as Goloka Dwarka, the eternal abode of Krishna in the spiritual realm.
