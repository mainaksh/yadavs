The Sacred System

What Is a Gotra? ‚Äî The Science of Lineage

Before exploring the clans, one must understand the system. Gotra (‡§ó‡•ã‡§§‡•ç‡§∞) is not merely a surname or clan name ‚Äî it is a precise genealogical-spiritual identifier that locates every Hindu within the cosmic web of ancestor-descent. Understanding gotra is essential to understanding why the Yadava clans are organised as they are.

The Meaning and Function of Gotra

The word Gotra derives from Sanskrit: go (cow) + tra (protector) ‚Äî literally "protector of the cow-pen," referring to the ancestral family unit of the Vedic age that shared a common cow pen. Over time it evolved to mean the patrilineal descent group tracing back to a common Rishi ancestor.
Every Hindu's gotra identifies their line of descent from one of the original Saptarishis (Seven Sages) or their principal disciples. The gotra functions as a complete identity record: it tells you your Vedic ancestor, your patrilineal line, who you may not marry (exogamy ‚Äî gotra must be different for marriage), and what ritual obligations you carry from your ancestor.
For Yadavas specifically, gotras connect back through the mortal royal lines to the Saptarishis ‚Äî and ultimately through them to Brahma. When a Yadava says their gotra is "Kashyapa," they are declaring descent (through many intermediate generations) from the great Rishi Kashyapa ‚Äî one of Brahma's sons. This is not metaphor; it is sacred genealogical record.

The Pravara ‚Äî The Three Sacred Ancestors

Every gotra comes with a Pravara ‚Äî a recitation of 3, 5, or 7 ancestral sages who mark the key nodes in one's lineage. In Vedic rituals, the officiant introduces himself with his gotra and pravara: "I am of the Kashyapa gotra, of the Kashyapa-Avatsara-Naidhruva pravara". This identifies not just the root ancestor but the specific sub-branch within the gotra. Yadava communities maintain these pravaras with great care in their ritual tradition.
The Three Rules of Gotra
  • Exogamy ‚Äî One may never marry within the same gotra. The gotra identifies a patrilineal descent group; marrying within it would be marrying a relative. This rule is inviolable in traditional Yadava communities.
  • Patrilineal transmission ‚Äî Gotra passes from father to children (not from mother). A woman takes her husband's gotra at marriage. This is why Hindu brides are said to "enter" a new gotra upon marriage.
  • Ritual identity ‚Äî In all Vedic rituals ‚Äî yajna, shraddha (ancestor worship), marriage, upanayana ‚Äî one's gotra is announced. It connects the individual ritual to the cosmic chain of ancestor-descent.

Why Atri-Gotra Is Central to Yadavas

The most important gotra for understanding the Yadava genealogy is the Atri Gotra ‚Äî because the entire Chandravansha is ultimately of Atri's descent. Chandra (the Moon God) was born of Atri's tapas. Budha was Chandra's son. Pururavas was Budha's son. Yadu was Pururavas's descendant. So all Yadavas, at the deepest level, trace their spiritual ancestry to Atri Maharishi ‚Äî one of the Saptarishis, born from Brahma's eyes. This is why many Yadava sub-communities use the Atri gotra as their primary gotra.
